# 2. PATTERNS & INSIGHTS

## Insight 1: AI Has Eaten the Accelerator World

- **YC W25-W26**: 88.5% of 793 companies are AI-related
- **a16z Speedrun SR004**: 69% AI-native
- **Antler India**: 7/11 recent companies in AI/deeptech
- **Sequoia Arc**: ~30% pure AI/ML, but AI touches most other categories too

**What this means for you:** If you're NOT building something AI-related, your odds drop significantly at most top accelerators. The exceptions are Boost VC (deep tech thesis), Techstars (vertical programs in non-AI areas), and StartupWiseGuys (B2B verticals).

## Insight 2: B2B Massively Outweighs B2C

- YC: 66% B2B vs 7% pure consumer
- Most accelerators show similar patterns
- Consumer plays need viral/growth evidence to compensate

## Insight 3: The "Ex-FAANG + AI Agent" Profile Dominates YC

- 16.6% of YC founders are ex-FAANG
- 10.1% are Ivy League graduates
- 10.1% are repeat YC founders
- This creates a profile archetype -- but it's not the ONLY path in

## Insight 4: Repeat Applications Work

- YC, Sequoia Arc, 500 Global, and Antler all explicitly encourage reapplication
- Several notable alumni were rejected on first attempt
- Show measurable progress between applications

## Insight 5: Solo Founders Are Increasingly Viable

- YC: 14.4% solo founders (114 companies in W25-W26)
- EF and Antler are PURPOSE-BUILT for solo founders
- SPC explicitly welcomes solo founders who can build
- Key: solo founders must demonstrate stronger technical skills and traction

## Insight 6: International Founders Are Welcome But SF Dominates

- YC: 57% of companies are SF-based
- a16z Speedrun: 30+ countries represented, but MUST attend in SF
- SPC: Has Bangalore office (rare!)
- EF: Full Bangalore program
- Antler: Strong India presence (550+ individuals backed)

## Insight 7: The Application Video/Demo Is Make-or-Break

- AngelPad: Video pitch is "90% of the success"
- Techstars: Requires 3 videos (product demo, pitch deck walkthrough, team intro)
- YC: Product demo/video heavily influences interview invitations
- a16z: Demo and metrics are primary screening criteria

## Insight 8: Warm Intros Matter (Especially at Certain Programs)

- Boost VC: **90% of deals come through referrals**
- Seedcamp: Referrals from network significantly help
- YC and Techstars: Cold applications work, but referrals still help
- EF and Antler: Designed for cold applications (no referral needed)

## Insight 9: Post-Program Success Rates Vary Significantly

| Accelerator | Follow-on Funding Rate | Series A Conversion |
|-------------|----------------------|---------------------|
| YC | Highest (network effect) | ~50%+ |
| Techstars | 75% | 40% |
| 500 Global | Good | 30% |
| EF | Moderate (1/3 get funded, then ~50% raise seed) | Lower |
| Antler | 20-45% get initial investment post-residency | Varies |

## Insight 10: Equity and Funding Are NOT the Whole Picture

The real value ranking (beyond money):
1. **YC** -- Unmatched alumni network (5,000+ companies), brand signal, investor access
2. **a16z Speedrun** -- $5M+ in credits, a16z operator support, platform access
3. **Sequoia Arc** -- Sequoia portfolio company status, Ampersand network
4. **SPC** -- $900K+ in credits, community, no-pressure exploration
5. **Techstars** -- Mentor network, corporate partnerships
6. **Seedcamp** -- Expert Collective (Revolut/Wise/Stripe alumni operators)

## 7.4 Idea Validation Frameworks

### Framework 1: Minimum Viable Testing (MVT)
**Source:** Gagan Biyani (Maven founder) via First Round Review
**URL:** https://review.firstround.com/the-minimum-viable-testing-process-for-evaluating-startup-ideas

Run 3-5 MVTs BEFORE building an MVP. Each MVT tests one specific hypothesis. Force real commitment (payment or significant time investment) -- not surveys.

### Framework 2: The Idea Maze
**Source:** Chris Dixon (a16z partner)
**URL:** https://cdixon.org/2013/08/04/the-idea-maze

Map historical attempts, analogies, and theoretical frameworks to anticipate paths.

### Framework 3: Paul Graham's Filters
From https://paulgraham.com/startupideas.html:
- Is this a problem you personally have?
- Can you build a first version yourself?
- Do few others realize this is valuable?
- Who wants this SO much they'll use a crappy v1?
- Is the market growing fast?

### Framework 4: The Mom Test
**Book:** https://www.momtestbook.com/
Talk about their life, not your idea. Ask about specific past behavior, not hypothetical futures.

## 7.6 Solo Founder Friendly Idea Categories

**Best categories for one person to build:**

1. **Developer Tools & CLI Utilities** -- Low support burden, distribute via GitHub/HN/PH. Current trend: AI-powered dev tools.

2. **Vertical Micro-SaaS** -- Solve one specific problem for one specific industry (scheduling for dentists, inventory for breweries, invoicing for photographers). Lower competition, customers pay for specialization.

3. **AI-Powered Automation** -- Leverage LLMs to replace manual processes. High perceived value. Hot categories: document processing, data extraction, code review.

4. **API-First Products** -- Self-serve onboarding, usage-based pricing, scales with customers.

5. **Content/SEO-Driven SaaS** -- Tools that generate organic search traffic. Low-touch sales.

6. **Niche Marketplaces** -- Curated job boards, service directories. Start with one side curated (RemoteOK model).

7. **Productized Services** -- Fixed-price, defined-scope offerings. AI can automate increasing portions over time.

### Solo Founder Anti-Patterns (AVOID):
- Enterprise sales with long cycles
- Two-sided marketplaces requiring simultaneous supply/demand
- Hardware products (manufacturing/logistics/inventory)
- Consumer social apps (need massive user acquisition)
- Highly regulated industries without domain expertise

## 7.7 The Quick-Start Checklist

1. Read Paul Graham's "How to Get Startup Ideas" (https://paulgraham.com/startupideas.html)
2. Watch YC Startup School talks on evaluating ideas (https://www.startupschool.org/curriculum)
3. Review YC's current RFS (https://www.ycombinator.com/rfs) and a16z Big Ideas (https://a16z.com/big-ideas-in-tech-2025/)
4. List 10 problems you personally experience daily
5. List 10 broken tools/processes in your professional domain
6. Read 50+ negative reviews on G2/Capterra in your target category
7. Check Google Trends for demand validation on your top 3 ideas
8. Check Exploding Topics for emerging trends in your space
9. Search Hacker News and Reddit for discussions about your problem
10. Run 1-2 MVTs (Minimum Viable Tests) before writing code
11. Talk to 10+ potential customers using The Mom Test methodology
12. Check Crunchbase for existing competitors and their funding
13. Build the simplest version that solves the core problem
14. Launch on Product Hunt, Show HN, or relevant community
15. Measure: Will people PAY? (Not just "like" or "sign up")
16. Apply to 3-5 accelerators simultaneously
